Biodiversity

Biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth. This includes all organisms, species, and populations. It also includes the genetic variation among these; and their complex communities and ecosystems [1].

Why is it important?

It is essential for processes which support all life on Earth. Without a wide range of animals, plants, and other organisms we would not have the ecosystems which we rely on to provide us with air and food [2].



























Madagascar

Biodiversity in Madagascar

80% of Madagascar's plants and animals are only found in the country. However, these are in danger from deforestation, hunting and illegal trade in wildlife [3]. The country has lost more than 80% of its original forest cover. This is made worse when the countries high level of endemism is taken into account as it means one hectare of forest loss has a larger effect on biodiversity in Madagascar than forest loss anywhere else in the world [4].

What effects the biodiversity in the Madagascar?

It is affected mainly by destructive human practices such as the clearing of natural habitats (which accounts for 0.55% a year) as well as the overexplotation of natural resources and hunting. The impact of climate change on biodiversity has also become apparent especially in the marine and coastal enviroments [4].

  • Deforestation:

    • It is caused by the expansion of agriculture which is driven by an increase in population size, issues relating to energy needs such as charcoal production, forest and brush fires, mining, and poverty [4].


  • Illegal wildlife trade

    • Loss of habitat leave Madagascar's unique species exposed to being targeted by traffickers. Plants and animals such as the rosewood trees, tortoises, chameleons, geckos, and snakes are the most targeted species in Madagascar [5].



























Protecting Biodiversity in Madagascar

Madagascar has over 100 protected areas with various IUCN status which cover around 10% of terrestrial and seascapes and are managed by the Madagascar National Parks association [5]. The most prominent Strict Nature Reserves is the "Tsingy de Bemaraha Reserve" which spans around 210,000 km2. The area north of the Bemaraha National Park is reserved for scientific research and conservation of the highly endemic, diverse flora and fauna spanning the western coastal and inland transitional savanna ecosystems. However, the dominant National Park is a lot smaller. The "Amber Mountain National Park" is only 44,973 km2. This high altitude rain forest offers a cool, refreshing contrast to the hot plains surrounding the mountain. Countless waterfalls and streams cascade down the park's slopes and into its five lakes, providing drinking water for Antsiranana. The park is home to over 1,000 plant species and numerous lemur, amphibian and reptile species [6, 7, 8].
